01 Why This Matters

We are building the most transformative technology in human history. We should probably do the math.

AI will generate extraordinary abundance — lower prices, new discoveries, productivity beyond anything we've seen. But the transition period between "AI augments workers" and "AI replaces entire job categories" contains a set of economic risks that we can only navigate if we see them clearly, early enough to act.

01

The displacement will be uneven

A junior copywriter and a master electrician face wildly different automation timelines. Good policy requires understanding which occupations are affected, when, and why — not treating "AI" as a monolithic force.

02

Speed matters more than scale

The gap between meaningful displacement and full displacement in any sector will be small. Once AI is better, faster, cheaper, and safer than humans at a task, competitive pressure drives near-total adoption within years, not decades.

03

The transition has a tipping point

There is a specific moment where falling wages and employment outpace AI-driven price reductions. Before that point, the economy flourishes. After it, a self-reinforcing contraction begins. We need to find that moment — and act before it arrives.

The purpose of ATLAS is not to predict doom. It is to find the precise policy window — the years we have to implement the right mix of wage supports, asset ownership, and transfer payments — so that the extraordinary abundance AI creates is shared widely enough to sustain a thriving economy and a thriving society.

Every major economic transition in history — industrialization, electrification, computerization — created enormous net benefits for humanity. But in every case, the transition period itself caused real suffering for real people. The difference this time is speed. Previous transitions unfolded over generations. This one may unfold over a single decade.

That compression means we don't have the luxury of waiting to see what happens. By the time displacement is obvious, the policy window may have closed.

ATLAS exists to give policymakers, researchers, and the public a rigorous, transparent tool for testing their assumptions. What happens if autonomous vehicles arrive in 2028? What if robotics lags by five years? How large does a sovereign wealth fund need to be to prevent demand collapse? What transfer payment level stays within the inflation-neutral zone?

These aren't rhetorical questions. They have mathematical answers. This tool computes them.

02 The BFCS Framework

Adoption isn't about capability alone. It's about clearing four thresholds simultaneously.

Most AI-labor models treat automation as a binary: if AI can do a task, the job disappears. Reality is far more complex. Autonomous vehicles are already ten times safer than human drivers — but that's not enough for regulatory approval, public trust, or fleet deployment at scale.

ATLAS uses the BFCS framework: for any occupation, AI must prove it is Better, Faster, Cheaper, and Safer than the human alternative — and what "enough" means for each dimension varies enormously by sector, role, and seniority level.

A junior software developer has a "Safer" threshold of about 0.7 — errors are catchable in code review. A cardiac surgeon's "Safer" threshold is 0.999. This single insight changes automation timeline predictions by decades.

Once all four thresholds are met, however, adoption is rapid. The first companies to adopt gain an insurmountable cost advantage, driving competitors to automate or go bankrupt. The gap between "meaningful displacement begins" and "full displacement" is narrow — especially for software-deployed AI.

03 How the Model Works

Every assumption is explicit. Every formula is inspectable. Every parameter is adjustable.

ATLAS is not a black box. Below is the complete methodology — every equation, every choice, every data source. We publish this so you can challenge our math, adjust our assumptions, and build your own understanding of what's coming.

M-01

Eight Capability Vectors

We decompose "AI" into eight distinct technology vectors, each with its own capability trajectory, deployment constraints, and occupational impact profile. This matters because "AI can do X" means nothing without specifying which kind of AI, deployed how, at what scale.

Each vector follows a modified sigmoid trajectory over time, parameterized by its current floor, maximum ceiling, improvement steepness, and inflection year. You control all four parameters per vector.

// Capability score for vector c at year t Sc(t) = Sfloor + (Sceil − Sfloor) / (1 + e−k(t − tmid)) // Where: // S_floor = current capability (today) // S_ceil = maximum achievable capability // k = steepness of improvement // t_mid = year of inflection (fastest improvement)

For robotics baselines, we anchor to Tesla Optimus production milestones and capability demonstrations. For autonomous vehicles, we use Tesla's Cybercab, Semi, and Full Self-Driving fleet data exclusively — because scale of manufacturing, not lab demos, determines actual deployment timelines. Software-deployed AI (language, code, agentic) has a much steeper trajectory because deployment requires only inference compute, not physical manufacturing.

We map the U.S. labor force into 51 occupation clusters spanning 15 industry categories, from software engineering to agriculture. Within each cluster, we differentiate by role and seniority — because a junior software developer and a staff architect face fundamentally different automation timelines.

Each cluster maps directly to Bureau of Labor Statistics Standard Occupational Classification (SOC) codes, ensuring our employment and wage baselines come from real federal data, not estimates.

Why separate education and healthcare administration? In an AI-enabled future, administrative overhead in education and healthcare represents cost bloat that can and should be eliminated by policy. Teachers and doctors stay; the bureaucratic layer around them goes. The model reflects this by giving administrators a headcount-to-productivity ratio of 1.0 (every automated task removes a person) while teachers get 0.1 (AI tutors augment, not replace).

M-03

Adoption S-Curves & Competitive Pressure

Once all four BFCS thresholds are met for an occupation-role, adoption follows a logistic S-curve — but the shape of that curve varies dramatically by deployment type.

Software-deployed AI (language models, code agents, decision systems) has a nearly vertical S-curve because deployment requires only spinning up inference. Robotics follows a gradual curve constrained by manufacturing throughput and supply chain capacity. Autonomous vehicles fall in between, gated by regulatory approval timelines and fleet conversion rates.

// Adoption rate for occupation o, role r, at year t α(o,r,t) = 1 / (1 + e−a(t − ttrigger − lag)) // a = steepness: // Software: 2.0–4.0 (near-vertical) // Robotics: 0.5–1.0 (gradual, manufacturing-constrained) // AV: 0.3–1.5 (regulatory-gated)

A critical accelerant: once adoption exceeds 20% in any sector, remaining non-adopters face a mounting cost disadvantage. Early adopters can undercut on price, forcing competitors to automate or face bankruptcy. We model this as a competitive pressure multiplier that steepens the S-curve's tail.

// Competitive pressure makes the gap between // "meaningful" and "full" displacement narrow pressure(o,t) = max(0, α(o,t) − 0.2) × multiplier αadj(o,t) = α(o,t) × (1 + pressure(o,t))

For robotics and autonomous vehicles, a geopolitical risk modifier can flatten the curve further. Supply chain disruptions, trade restrictions, or rare earth material shortages would slow physical AI deployment even after capability thresholds are met.

M-04

Task Erosion to Occupation Displacement

Automation doesn't eliminate entire occupations overnight. It first erodes individual tasks within a role — we call this task erosion. A junior analyst might lose 60% of their tasks to AI while the remaining 40% (client relationships, ambiguous judgment calls) persists. But task erosion has consequences: fewer tasks per worker means fewer workers needed, and workers with weaker bargaining power earn lower wages.

// Task erosion — what fraction of work is automated τ(o,r,t) = α(o,r,t) × automatable_fraction(o,r) // Headcount reduction — firms don't cut 1:1, ratio is ~0.6–0.8 headcount(o,r,t) = baseline × (1 − τ × productivity_to_headcount_ratio) // Wage depression — even survivors earn less wage(o,r,t) = baseline_wage × (1 − τ × wage_elasticity)

Task erosion is an early warning indicator. When you see task erosion rising but headcounts holding steady, that's the "AI augments workers" phase. When headcounts start falling, displacement has begun. The model tracks both, letting you watch the transition unfold.

M-05

The Macro Feedback Loop & Tipping Point

This is the heart of ATLAS. We track Aggregate Real Purchasing Power (ARPP) — the total spending power of the population after accounting for price changes. ARPP is the single metric that determines whether the economy expands or contracts.

// Aggregate Real Purchasing Power ARPP(t) = [E(t)×W(t) + U(t)×T(t) + N×A(t)] / P(t) // E = employed, W = avg wage, U = unemployed // T = transfers, N = population, A = asset income // P = price level (falling due to AI deflation)

AI simultaneously pushes wages and employment down while also pushing prices down. Before the tipping point, the price decline outpaces the income decline — consumers are actually better off. Fewer people work, but everything is cheaper, and the math still works.

The tipping point is the exact year when income decline overtakes price decline:

// The tipping point T* occurs when: |d(E×W)/dt| > |d(P)/dt × demand(t−1)| // Before T*: economy grows (AI boom) // After T*: demand contraction begins

After the tipping point, a self-reinforcing displacement-demand feedback cycle engages: falling demand creates revenue pressure on firms, which accelerates their automation to cut costs, which displaces more workers, which further reduces demand. The model captures this as a feedback term that accelerates adoption rates when ARPP is declining.

M-06

New Job Creation vs. Automation Coverage

The strongest counterargument to labor displacement is: "AI will create new jobs we can't imagine." ATLAS tests this argument mathematically.

We define automation coverage A(t) — the fraction of all possible human tasks AI can perform. As AI capabilities expand across all eight vectors, A(t) approaches 1.0. Any new job created has a survivability proportional to (1 − A(t)), because as AI capability broadens, newly created roles are increasingly automatable at inception.

// Durable new jobs = new roles × probability they survive durable_new_jobs(t) = Jnew(t) × (1 − A(t))p // As A(t) → 0.9+, new job survivability → near zero // regardless of how many new roles are created

The mathematical insight: Even if AI-driven R&D creates an explosion of new industries, if automation coverage is 90%+ by the time those industries mature, virtually every job they generate is born automatable. The "new jobs" argument only holds if job creation outpaces capability expansion — and the empirical trend (capabilities expanding exponentially, job categories emerging linearly) suggests it won't. The model lets you see exactly how implausibly large new job creation would need to be to offset displacement.

M-07

Money Supply, Deflation & the Net Neutral Zone

Government transfer payments must be funded. Whether through taxation of AI company profits or money creation, funding transfers risks inflation. But AI simultaneously creates deflation through productivity gains and declining inference costs. The interaction between these forces determines how much the government can spend without destabilizing prices.

// Fisher equation: money supply × velocity = price × output M(t) × V(t) = P(t) × Y(t) // The "net neutral zone" — max transfers with zero net inflation: ΔMmax = (Ptarget × ΔYai − ΔPai × Y) / V // If AI deflation > transfer inflation → money is free // If AI deflation < transfer inflation → constraint binds

This is possibly the most important output of the model: under many scenarios, AI's deflationary effect is so large that substantial transfer payments can be funded without any net inflation. The real economy absorbs the money supply expansion because AI is simultaneously expanding output. ATLAS calculates exactly where that boundary is.

M-08

Second-Order Employment Multipliers

When a major employment category is automated, the impact extends far beyond the directly displaced workers. Three million automated truckers means fewer customers at truck stops, diners, motels, and fuel stations along freight corridors. Automated office workers mean fewer lunch orders at downtown restaurants, less demand for dry cleaning, reduced commercial real estate occupancy.

We use Bureau of Economic Analysis input-output employment multipliers and academic estimates to capture these cascading effects:

Sector Multiplier Source
Trucking 3.4× American Trucking Associations
Technology 4.3× BEA / high local spending multiplier
Construction 2.4× BEA input-output tables
Healthcare 2.1× BEA / local economic anchor effect
Manufacturing 1.6× Moretti (2010)
Retail 1.2× BEA / already local service

These multipliers mean that the total employment impact of automation is substantially larger than direct displacement alone. When ATLAS shows 3 million trucking jobs lost, the real impact is closer to 10 million when adjacent employment is included.

M-09

Real Data, No Estimates

ATLAS uses the Bureau of Labor Statistics Public Data API (v2) for all employment and wage baselines. This includes Occupational Employment and Wage Statistics (OEWS) for ~830 detailed occupations across all 50 states, Current Employment Statistics (CES) for monthly industry-level employment tracking, Local Area Unemployment Statistics (LAUS) for state and metro area unemployment rates, and Consumer Price Index (CPI) for historical price level data.

The model also integrates Bureau of Economic Analysis data for GDP computations and employment multipliers, and Federal Reserve Economic Data (FRED) for money supply and velocity of money.

All BLS data is fetched from the live API. No data is prepopulated, estimated, or assumed. If the data hasn't been loaded yet, the model shows empty states — not fake numbers. You bring your own BLS API key (free from the Bureau of Labor Statistics) and the model populates with real, current federal employment data.

Feedback Cycle

The displacement-demand feedback cycle is not inevitable. It's a specific failure mode that occurs when the transition from "AI augments workers" to "AI replaces workers" happens faster than policy can respond. The cycle becomes self-reinforcing because falling demand creates revenue pressure, which makes automation more attractive, which displaces more workers.

But every link in this chain is breakable. Asset ownership means displaced workers still have income through dividends. Transfer payments maintain demand. Wage subsidies slow the automation incentive. The question isn't whether to intervene — it's how much, when, and through which channel.

ATLAS calculates the exact year when each intervention must be in place to prevent the loop from engaging. This "policy window" is the most critical output of the model: it tells policymakers not just what to do, but how much time they have to do it.

Under most scenarios, the policy window is measured in years, not decades. But it exists. And if we use it wisely, the outcome isn't doom — it's an economy where AI-generated abundance is distributed widely enough that aggregate demand is sustained, innovation continues, and human welfare improves across the board.
